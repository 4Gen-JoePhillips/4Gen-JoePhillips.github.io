Background

Joseph Phillips II.
Fourth generation.

The Phillips family has been in oil and gas since before the industry had a name for what they did. That heritage carries with it a particular way of thinking about capital — patient, long-duration, grounded in the substance of what lies beneath the surface rather than the noise above it.

Joseph Phillips II is a proven exploration oil finder with a career spanning multiple basins across Canada, the United Kingdom, and the North Sea. That work was never administrative. It required the integration of deep geological and geophysical expertise — reading stratigraphy, interpreting seismic data, understanding the structural and depositional conditions that concentrate hydrocarbons into commercially significant accumulations. The discipline demands a particular quality of attention: the ability to look at a basin that the market has passed over, identify what the conventional interpretation missed, and hold a thesis long enough for the geology to confirm it.

The discoveries that matter in exploration are rarely found in the obvious places. They emerge from a willingness to enter frontier basins before consensus forms — to synthesise geological, geophysical, and geochemical data into a coherent picture of where untapped resources lie, and to act on that picture before others have arrived at the same conclusion. Across Western Canada and the UK, that approach produced new pool discoveries, significant reserve additions, and positions in plays that had not previously been recognised as prospective. The Central North Sea promote licences offsetting Cygnus were not acquired by accident. They were the product of a considered geological view about structural continuity in an underexplored fairway.

That generational approach — rigorous, evidence-based, willing to hold a view that the consensus has not yet reached — is precisely what Joe Phillips brings to biotechnology. The disciplines are different. The intellectual method is the same. Where an exploration geologist interrogates subsurface data to identify where value is buried and unrecognised, a serious biotech investor interrogates clinical and mechanistic data to identify where scientific reality has outpaced market understanding. Both require deep expertise. Both require the patience to be early.

The past decade has been given to building that expertise in biology — not as a tourist, but with the same systematic rigour brought to every basin entered and every licence negotiated. 4Gen Capital is the vehicle for that work. It is a private family office. It does not manage outside capital.

The Phillips family history in oil and gas precedes the formation of most of the companies that now define the industry. That lineage is not a credential to be presented — it is simply the condition under which everything else has been built.

Across the family's operating history, the focus has been consistent: find the resource, structure the deal, build the relationships, and hold through the cycle. The work in the United Kingdom spans two distinct vehicles and multiple licensing rounds — from the onshore Lincolnshire discoveries of the AltaQuest era to the offshore Central North Sea positions awarded to 4Gen Energy UK Limited.

Investment Thesis

Public life sciences.
Studied before bought.

The transition from energy to life sciences is not a departure from the family's investment philosophy — it is an extension of it. Both disciplines reward those who understand the underlying resource before the market does.

The specific opportunity lies in dislocated value: companies where mechanism of action has been established through clinical data — where the science has spoken — and yet the public markets have not yet recognised what the evidence implies. That gap, between demonstrated biology and market valuation, is where positions are built.

The search is for companies with established biomarkers, proven mechanistic pathways, and clinical evidence of durable response — particularly where the data points toward long-term remission rather than disease management. The evolution of the field is followed closely: from the first autologous CAR-T approvals in oncology, through the emerging allogeneic platforms that address the scalability and access constraints of the original modality, and into the next generation of small molecule approaches — including molecular glue degraders — that could extend transformative treatment to a far broader patient population at a fraction of the cost.

There is no outside capital. There are no fund mechanics. There is only a considered view, a long time horizon, and the patience that comes from four generations of knowing what it means to wait for something buried to surface.

  • The focus is companies where mechanism of action has been clinically established and biomarkers validated — but where the public market has not yet priced what the evidence implies. The dislocation, not the speculation, is the entry point.

  • Capital is deployed where proof of mechanism already exists — Phase I/II data demonstrating durable response, validated biomarker stratification, or a mechanistic readthrough from an adjacent approved programme. The science has spoken; the market has not yet listened.

  • Particular interest in platforms with clinical evidence pointing toward long-term remission — most compelling in CAR-T oncology, where established biomarker correlates now illuminate a parallel path in autoimmune disease. A treatment that addresses the disease rather than suppresses the symptom commands a different kind of attention.

  • The trajectory from autologous CAR-T to allogeneic platforms to small molecule degraders is followed with intent. Each step represents an expansion of the addressable patient population and a reduction in treatment cost. A molecular glue that achieves what a bespoke cell therapy achieves — at scale, affordably — is not an incremental development. It is a different order of impact entirely.

Scientific Focus

The modalities under
active study.

01

Genetic Medicine

Base editing, prime editing, and CRISPR-based platforms addressing disease at the sequence level. Focus on companies building modular engines across multiple therapeutic areas rather than single-indication programmes.

Base Editing · Prime Editing · CRISPR
02

Molecular Glue Degraders

Small molecules that induce proximity between a target protein and an E3 ubiquitin ligase, directing the cell's own machinery to degrade the target. A rapidly maturing modality with the potential to address proteins previously considered undruggable.

Targeted Degradation · E3 Ligase · PROTAC
03

Cell Therapy

Engineered immune cell platforms — CAR-T, NK, and iPSC-derived formats — with particular interest in allogeneic approaches and next-generation persistence and manufacturing profiles.

CAR-T · NK Cell · Allogeneic
04

Nucleic Acid Platforms

mRNA therapeutics, siRNA, and antisense oligonucleotides. The delivery problem — specifically extrahepatic LNP and non-viral vector innovation — is where the most consequential scientific work is currently occurring.

mRNA · siRNA · ASO · LNP
05

Immunology & Autoimmune

Mechanistically grounded approaches to autoimmune and inflammatory disease that aim at durable tolerance rather than chronic suppression. The field is at an early but accelerating inflection.

Tolerance · Inflammation · Immune Regulation
06

Precision Oncology

Biomarker-stratified therapeutics — ADCs, targeted small molecules, and IO combinations — where mechanistic clarity supports a thesis on durable clinical benefit rather than statistical noise.

ADC · IO · Targeted Therapy
